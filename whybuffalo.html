Why Grass Fed Buffalo Meat?

Buffalo are eating their natural diet
A buffalo's traditional habitat is an open prairie. Prairie meadow is not the same as a field of a single grain grown for feed. Meadows host a natural mix of trees, grasses, wildflowers and other edible plant life for the buffalo to graze on. When a buffalo ranges freely it will graze in the early morning and evening when temperatures coolest and there are less distractions (flies and such are more active during midday), and apparently the the grass tastes sweeter. On a typical warm summer afternoon you will find the buffalo in the shadest area of the range catching a little shut eye just like most humans do.

When buffalo feed on grass, the meat tastes different then when they have been fed grain. The grass fed meat is slightly sweeter, with no bland fatty after taste whichis often associated with grain-fed bison, a product that actually tasts a lot like most store bought beef. Grass fed buffalo have Omega acid ratios that are healthy the ratio of Omega 6 to Omaga 3 fatty acid is 4:1 or less.

Grass Fed Buffalo Management

Grass-fed buffalo are raised in pastures and are never exposed to management practices which include feedlots or feedlot finishing.

The ideal grass-fed buffalo diet is approximately 93% grass, 5% forbs (alfalfa, clover, wildflowers etc.), and 2% browse (leaves from woody plants). Mineral supplements can be part of their diet, especially over the winter months. On occasion, buffalo may be fed some oats or other grains as an enticement to move from one pasture to another. But under no circumstances are grains (corn, wheat, barley, oats etc.) a staple in the grass-fed buffalo's diet. In addition, best practices protocol does not allow for the use of antibiotics, growth hormones, or chemical parasite control.

Health benefits from grass-fed buffalo are best achieved when buffalo graze on green leafy grasses, or where available, dormant pasture during the winter. The same health benefits are not realized when their diet consists of hay or silage. That's why it is always best to purchase bison meat coming from an animal that has been harvested coming off pasture, meaning from June to November for most of North America.

Nutritional Value
Grass fed buffalo is lower in total fat, especially saturated fat than all other meats including grain fed buffalo. It is lower in cholestrol and calories. Grass fed buffalo is considerably higher in beta-carotene and conjugated linoleic acid (CLA) something that can not be said of grain fed animals. Beta-cartene is used in the body to make viatmin A, a powerful anti-oxidant linked to preventing cancer and cardio-vascular disease. It can also give grass-fed bison fat a slightly yellow color; something that the grain-driven part of the buffalo industry will sometimes erronously play up as a negative factor. Grain-fed buffalo has white fat, much like you see in most if not all, supermarket beef. In prelimiary studies CLA, a fatty acid has been linked to reducing the risk of cancer, diabetes and obesity.
